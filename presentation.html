Literature Review

Evolution of Noise Reduction

1979 — Spectral Subtraction Boll, IEEE

First frequency-domain noise removal. Simple but introduces musical noise artifacts.

1990s — Wavelet Denoising & Wiener Filters

MMSE-optimal filtering and multi-resolution time-frequency decomposition.

2010s — Deep Neural Network Denoisers

High accuracy but require GPU, large models, and massive training data — impractical for edge.

2020s — Lightweight Hybrid Approaches

Combining classical DSP with intelligent VAD for resource-constrained platforms.

2026 — Our Proposed VGDWF ★

Dual-feature VAD + dynamic noise PSD + adaptive β(t). No GPU needed. Runs on RPi 4.

Methodology — Core Contribution

VAD-Guided Dynamic Wiener Filter

STEP 1

Dual-Feature VAD

Classify each frame using Log-Energy + Spectral Entropy. Speech = high energy AND low entropy.

STEP 2

Dynamic Noise PSD Update

During silence frames: update noise estimate with exponential average. During speech frames: freeze noise estimate.

STEP 3

Adaptive β(t) Oversubtraction

Low SNR → β = 3.0 (aggressive). High SNR → β = 1.0 (preserve speech). Sigmoid-mapped per frame.

STEP 4

Wiener Gain Application

H(f,t) = max(|Y|² - β·Pnn, 0) / |Y|² → smooth multiplicative gain, minimal musical noise.

// VAD Decision
Speech = (E_t > θ_E) AND (H_t < θ_H)
// Noise PSD Update
P_nn(t) = α · P_nn(t-1) + (1-α) · |Y(f,t)|²
// Adaptive Beta
β(t) = β_max − (SNR_t+5)/25 · (β_max − β_min)
// Wiener Gain
H(f,t) = max(|Y|² − β·P_nn, 0) / |Y|²

★ Key Innovation: Continuous noise tracking without speech contamination

Results & Discussion

Stationary vs. Non-Stationary Noise

Performance at -5dB SNR (Hardest Condition)

Stationary Noise (White, Fan)

Traditional filters perform reasonably well because noise profile is constant. VGDWF still leads by 10-15% due to adaptive β(t).

Non-Stationary Noise (Street, Cafe) ★

Static methods collapse — their initial noise profile becomes obsolete. VGDWF's dynamic noise tracking produces ~20% improvement in Cafe noise at -5dB.

Butterworth Limitation

Cannot suppress in-band noise (80Hz–4kHz). Fan hum and babble fall directly within the speech band.
